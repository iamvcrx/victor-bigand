L’odyssée du cerveau

2023-24

This theater show (which translates as « Brain odyssey ») explains how the brain reacts to music by presenting how some of its main structures work (auditoral cortex, basilar membrane…). While presenting those structures, a physical representation of the brain lights up reacting to music in real time. I am in charge of the real-time audio processing and real-time light generation algorithms.

This project is led and written by Emmanuel BIGAND, a neuroscientific researcher working on how the brain reacts to music. A professionnal string quartet (the Rolling String Quartet) plays music live and the show is presented by the talented singer Amélia Donnier. The theater light creation is made by Gérald Karlikow.

The project has already started and received really good feedbacks from the audience (Seine Musicale in Paris, Saint-Priest theater in Lyon, Grenier à sel in Avignon…).

Research : active control of loudspeaker stands

2023

During my master degree internship, I worked in S3AM’s team of IRCAM STMS laboratory on active control of loudspeaker stands (supervised by David Roze and Henri Boutin). The goal was to reduce vibrations transfered by the speaker to its environment to avoid vibration related issues (neighboors, degradation in museums or in concert places).

We worked on a physical and numerical modelisation of a loudspeaker and it front plate vibration structure. We used Port Hamiltonian formalism for further work on the subject (non linearities, other system interconnections…). We used finite element method to predicts how the structure would vibrate given the voltage input of the loudspeaker. We used those predictions to inform an active control feed forward LMS algorithm during experiences. We discussed the contribution of the model prediction in the speed and quality of reduction, compared to a case without any modelisation.

JYMMIN : music for rehabilitation

2021

« JYMMIN » is a project developed by Tom Fritz at Max Planck Institute in Leipzig to stimulate physical activity with music. We used it for a research on how active music in sport can help people suffering from drug or alcohol addictions to rehabilitate. We worked with the Laboratory for Research on Learning and Developing (LEAD) in Dijon in collaboration with the hospital center « La Chartreuse », supervised by Barbara Tillman.

In this project, I was responsible for the computer coding of musical variations in response to physical effort. In particular, I was responsible for composing variations of patterns and coding them into the system to motivate patients to go further in their effort.
